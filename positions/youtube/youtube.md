### WHAT YOU'RE BUILDING

Done [SHOW DIAGRAM OR SIMPLE SLIDE: SCHWAB → PYTHON SCRIPT → GOOGLE SHEETS]

There are three pieces to this.

First, Schwab. You'll download your full transaction history as a CSV — every stock,
every buy and sell, every option trade, every dividend, going back as far as you have history.
One export, all your positions.

Second, a Python script. I didn't write this script myself — Claude Code wrote it for me based on
conversations we had about what I wanted to track. It's about 1,200 lines of Python.
I'll point you to the GitHub repo where you can download it.
You don't need to understand the code or modify it.

Third, Google Sheets. The script reads your CSV, figures out what's open and what's closed, fetches
current prices from Yahoo Finance, and builds out your entire dashboard in a
multi-tab Google Sheet that lives in your Google Drive. 
It's Formatted, with formulas, organized by status — open positions, closed
positions, and drops anything it couldn't make sense of in another tab.

### SECURITY

Done [STAY ON CAMERA OR SIMPLE SLIDE]

Before we get into the steps, I want to address something important:  Security.
Lets talk about what Claude does and doesn't have access to when you use this.

Claude Code is what I used to *build* the script. Those conversations happened on my machine, and
Claude helped me write and refine the code over several sessions. But Claude is not involved when
you *run* the script. When you type that one command, you're running a plain Python file on your own
computer. Claude isn't watching, Claude isn't reading your data, Claude has no connection to what
happens next.

Here's exactly what the script touches: it reads your Schwab CSV from your local drive. It calls
Yahoo Finance's public API to get current stock and option prices — that's public market data, no
authentication needed. And it writes to your own Google Sheet using OAuth credentials that you
create and control. Your spreadsheet ID, your credentials, your Google account.

Your financial data — your positions, your transaction history, your cost basis — never passes
through Anthropic's servers. It goes from your hard drive to your Google Drive. That's it.

The script is open source. You can read every line of it before you run it.

### STEP 2 — DOWNLOAD YOUR TRANSACTION HISTORY FROM SCHWAB

Done [SHOW SCHWAB WEBSITE]

Log in to Schwab, select the right account, then go to Transaction History.

Done [Date Range]

Set the date range to cover your full history — make sure 'All' is selected
to go back as far as you can, and hit search.
The script handles everything in one file, so you want all your transactions,
all your tickers, in a single export.

Done [SHOW EXPORT BUTTON]

Now we're ready to download it.  First make sure the date range shown here
is what you're expecting.  Click Export/Download icon here. Download it as a CSV.
Save it under whatever name you like.  `ALL_TRANS.csv` is the name the script
is expecting, but you can name it whatever you like and later change the name
in the script.  Save this transaction file in the same folder as the script.

That's your input file. You don't need to clean it up or reformat anything
— the script handles all of that.

### STEP 4 — RUN THE SCRIPT

[SHOW TERMINAL]
Now we're ready to run the script.
Open your favorite shell, powershell or command prompt,
navigate to the folder with the script and your CSV, and run:

```
python setup_tab.py ALL_TRANS.csv Schwab
```

The first time you run it, a browser window will open asking you to authorize access to your Google
Sheets account. Click Allow. After that it runs silently — you won't see that prompt again.

[SHOW TERMINAL OUTPUT SCROLLING]

The script will work through all your tickers alphabetically. For each one it fetches the current
stock price and any open option values from Yahoo Finance, then builds the full tab in your Google
Sheet. This could take a few minutes depending on how many positions you have.
It will probably hit Google's API rate limit, and pause for a minute before
automatically continuing — you can just leave it running.

Done [SHOW GOOGLE SHEET POPULATING IN BACKGROUND]

The script is still running in the background.
When it finishes, your multi-tab spreadsheet is built
And a Totals Row will show up here on your summary tabs.

### STEP 5 — TOUR THE OUTPUT

Done - [SHOW GOOGLE SHEET — SUMMARY TABS AT LEFT]

It's finished now, you can see the totals row here.
The spreadsheet has three summary tabs, followed by a tab for each position.

We're looking at the **Summary-Open**, the current tab, and it shows all your currently
active positions in one place — you have the Underlying Stock section, Then call and put stats,
and overall metrics. There's a Totals row at the bottom. 
This is your dashboard for all open positions.

Done [SHOW SUMMARY-CLOSED TAB]

**Summary-Closed** has the same layout for positions you've fully exited.
You can see how each one performed.

Done [SHOW SUMMARY-INCONSISTENT TAB]

**Summary-Inconsistent** is a short list of any tickers where the script detected something it
couldn't reconcile — like a close transaction with no matching open, or a share count that doesn't
add up. It shows you what the issue is so you can investigate.
Maybe you can find the missing transactions elsewhere, add them to the CSV and re-run the script.

Done [SHOW AN INDIVIDUAL POSITION TAB]

Now let's look at a position tab.
We're looking at my Schwab position from the Schwab Brokerage.

Done [ZOOM ON CURRENT VALUES]

At the top left, you see the last time you ran the script here.
The Stock Price and option market values come from Yahoo Finance — that's the live input.
Adjusted Cost Basis per Share is one of the more interesting metrics. Your brokerage shows you
what you paid for the shares. This number shows what you *effectively* paid after subtracting 
the net option premium and dividends you've collected. After a few years of covered calls 
and dividends, this number can be significantly lower than your purchase price.  Here it is
\$56 vs \$68 dollars paid per share.

Done [SCROLL RIGHT — STOCK POSITION AND RESULTS]

All these top rows are meant to be mostly only about your underlying stock.

Stock Position shows your current number of shares, average cost, 
current market value, and net cost of all your buys and sells of the stock.

Stock Results shows your gain in dollars and percent, how long you've held on a 
FIFO-weighted basis, and your annualized gain on just the stock alone — 
before options or dividends enter the picture.

Done [SCROLL DOWN — CALLS SECTION]

Call History Stats section rolls up every call trade across the entire history of this position.
Open Calls shows your current call if you have one.
And Open Call Metrics has additional details about your open calls —
TV Ann Yield is highlighted.
That's the annualized yield of the time value remaining on your open calls, relative to
the close-out value of the covered shares. 
It tells you how hard this option is still working for you right now.
When it drops low enough, it may be time to consider closing out the position or rolling.
5% is the threshold that will cause me to start considering a move.

Done [SCROLL DOWN — PUTS, INCOME, P&L, RETURNS]

Same structure for puts.  As you can see, I don't have any open puts for this position.


Income tracks total dividends and net option premium — 
this is the cash this position has thrown off beyond stock price movement.
P&L Breakdown shows how each piece — stock,
calls, puts, dividends — contributes to your total return. And Returns gives you the bottom line:
close-out value, annualized yield on your invested capital, and annualized yield on your close-out
value.

And then below are all the transactions it used to compute all these stats.
